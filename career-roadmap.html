The Playbook

Lessons by Decade

Where to put your energy at each stage of your career if you want a curve like this one.

2000 – 2009

20s — Build the Base

Learn fundamentals · Ship anything · Earn your first serious cert

Flunked out of college for a semester with a 1.9 GPA — came back and graduated. Self-taught PHP, Perl, and MySQL. Said yes to a forensics contract I wasn't qualified for. Studied CISSP on nights and weekends for two years — failed it the first attempt, passed the second. Took the promotion the moment the cert came in.

2010 – 2014

Early 30s — Pick Your Platform

Specialize in a tool/platform the business pays to protect. Go deep, not wide.

Went deep on Splunk, EnCase, and vulnerability management. Became the person my manager sent clients to. Took a lead role at a regulated bank to prove I could operate under audit pressure.

2014 – 2020

Mid 30s — Stack Credibility

Add code to your security skillset. Lateral into engineering if you have to.

Made the "lateral" move from Senior Security Engineer to Software Engineer. Built data pipelines and exec dashboards. Learned ServiceNow. Became an Architect by 2020.

2020 – Today

Late 30s+ — Monetize the Expertise

Go to the vendor. Then decide: keep building, or start deciding what gets built.

Joined Nuvolo at $170K, then ServiceNow itself at $320K+ total. Five years later, pivoted from engineering to product management to own the roadmap.

The North Star

The 3 Rules

Every job, I leveled up in both skill AND salary. I followed the latest industry trends and stayed current with technology. Your journey is your own and it will look different from mine — but the rules are the same.

1

Always Be Learning a New Skill

The second you stop learning, you start sliding. Pick one new thing per quarter — an AWS service, a scripting language, a framework, a cert — and actually finish it.

2

Become Invaluable to Your Manager

Promotions come from the one person who signs your review. Know what they're measured on, and make their number easier to hit.

3

Keep Earning New Certifications

Certs don't raise your pay by themselves — they unlock the room where the raise happens. Stack them steadily over decades.

Do those three things, and the title, the comp, and the opportunities will follow.
